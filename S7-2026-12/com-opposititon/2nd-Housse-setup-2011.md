The biggest points from the transcript:
The meeting was about the May 6, 2010 “Flash Crash.”
The SEC and CFTC were reviewing why markets briefly broke down and what rules could reduce the chance of another event.

Markets had become faster, more electronic, and more fragile.
Speakers stressed that trading had moved from humans on floors to algorithms and high-speed systems, creating new risks when volatility spikes.

Regulators needed better cross-market coordination.
Because futures, equities, ETFs, options, and single-stock futures are linked, the SEC and CFTC needed coordinated rules instead of isolated market-by-market fixes.

Circuit breakers and trading pauses were strongly supported.
The committee praised existing single-stock pauses and recommended expanding them to more listed equities, ETFs, options, and related products.

They recommended “limit up / limit down” protections.
This would prevent trades from occurring too far outside recent price bands, helping stop sudden extreme price moves before they cascade.

Pre-trade risk controls were a major theme.
Brokers, exchanges, and firms should have stronger controls to prevent bad or excessive algorithmic orders from entering the market.

High-frequency trading and mass order cancellation were debated.
Some members wanted stronger action on whether huge order flow and cancellations impose costs or become disruptive trading activity.

Liquidity was treated as unreliable in stress periods.
The committee questioned whether displayed liquidity is truly available when markets are under pressure, since many orders can disappear quickly.

Market data and audit trails needed major improvement.
They recommended a consolidated audit trail so regulators could reconstruct events faster and identify who was trading across fragmented markets.

The report was unanimously accepted.
   The advisory committee voted to submit its recommendations to the SEC and CFTC for further rulemaking and public comment.

In short: the meeting said modern electronic markets are efficient but fragile, and regulators need better circuit breakers, risk controls, liquidity transparency, high-frequency trading oversight, and cross-market data to prevent another Flash Crash.

CAT Remarks With Timestamps
01:45:00 - The committee moves into its final topic: information. The speaker says markets and regulatory responsibilities depend heavily on information.

01:45:00 - They recommend better reporting on liquidity and market imbalance at large venues. The concern is distinguishing “real liquidity” from merely displayed or notional liquidity.

01:45:00 - The committee recommends that the SEC move forward with a Consolidated Audit Trail for U.S. equity markets.

01:45:00 - They add two caveats for CAT: it should be pursued with urgency, but also with attention to cost-benefit.

01:46:01 - They say the SEC should not wait for a perfect design before acting. The idea is to get into the CAT “game” quickly rather than letting perfection delay implementation.

01:46:01 - They cite May 6, 2010 as an example of why CAT would be useful. The Flash Crash investigation showed how valuable a consolidated audit trail could be for reconstructing what happened.

01:46:01 - The recommendation also says the CFTC should enhance its own data collection for orders and executions, so the futures side has better order-level information too.

01:47:00 - A commissioner asks what data problems most hindered the May 6 review, especially in the futures markets, and whether mandatory trader “markers” would help.

01:48:00 - The response: both equity and futures markets were limited by a lack of granularity in audit trail data.

01:48:00 - For the SEC specifically, the issue was that key audit-trail information was largely handled by exchanges and FINRA, while the SEC needed more direct consolidated access.

01:49:01 - The speaker says electronic traders now play a major role in pricing, but existing systems were still focused more on traditional registered market participants.

01:49:01 - CAT should allow regulators to identify the actual people or firms affecting the market across fragmented venues, and do so quickly instead of piecing it together over long periods.

01:49:01 - Key point: it is not enough for the audit trail to be “consolidated”; it also needs granularity.

01:50:01 - Another speaker urges the SEC and CFTC to coordinate their audit trail responsibilities, because traders operate across both futures and equity markets.

01:50:01 - They suggest some kind of marker or identifier so regulators can trace a trader’s activity across both markets.

01:50:01 - A commissioner clarifies whether this means a unique trader identifier or classification by trading behavior.

01:51:00 - The response is cautious: identifiers for every participant could create a lot of complexity. The speaker suggests focusing first on the most active market participants, especially those heavily affecting market activity.

Bottom line: CAT was discussed as a necessary tool for quickly reconstructing market events, identifying who drove activity across fragmented markets, and improving regulatory visibility after events like the Flash Crash. The speakers emphasized urgency, granularity, cross-market coordination, and possibly trader identifiers for the most active participants.

Pre-Trade Control / Risk Control Remarks
00:06:01 - Gary Gensler frames the topic upfront.
He says he is interested in “pre-trade risk safeguards” and “effective testing of risk management controls” for algorithmic trading systems.

00:54:02 - Commissioner Chilton asks whether exchanges or another entity should test algorithms/HFT systems before or during use.
He compares it to a “Good Housekeeping seal of approval” and asks whether testing environments like CME’s could be used to see how programs behave.

00:55:02 - Chilton also asks about accountability when algorithms or HFT systems go wrong quickly.
He connects this to disruptive trading rules and asks whether there should be consequences for runaway algorithms or HFT activity.

00:57:01 - Main recommendation starts.
The committee supports the SEC’s “naked access rule making” and urges the SEC to work with FINRA and exchanges to test broker-dealer risk management controls and supervisory procedures.

00:57:01 - Same section: the committee says oversight is evolutionary but wants to make sure appropriate controls are actually in place.

00:58:00 - The committee recommends that the CFTC impose “strict supervisory requirements” on designated contract markets and futures commission merchants that “employ or sponsor” firms using algorithmic order-routing strategies.

00:58:00 - They also recommend that the CFTC and SEC review whether to restrict disruptive trading activities involving extremely large orders or strategies.

00:59:01 - Rick Ketchum says primary responsibility for understanding, monitoring, and testing should sit with the intermediaries that provide market access.
Regulators and SROs should verify whether that supervision is effective.

01:00:00 - Ketchum says requiring every algorithm to be preapproved would create a narrow funnel and could hurt competition and innovation.
Instead, firms should bear primary responsibility, with strict supervisory oversight from regulators and SROs.

01:01:00 - David Ruder says the broker-dealer risk-control recommendation should be read broadly.
It is not only a sponsored-access issue; broker-dealers doing algorithmic trading themselves also need supervisory and management controls.

01:02:04 - Brooksley Born says algorithmic and high-frequency trading pose special problems for orderly markets, especially high cancellation rates.
She says cancellations could be considered disruptive trading and should be examined carefully.

01:02:04 - Mary Schapiro raises the idea of “throttles” on order-execution algorithms, while noting that implementation may be difficult and industry may be better positioned than regulators to design them.

01:04:01 - A commissioner asks whether the advisory committee considered consequences or punishment if pre-trade functionality, speed bumps, or access requirements are breached.

01:05:00 - Ketchum responds that existing disciplinary tools could allow fines or even limits on business for firms that fail in their responsibilities.

01:06:00 - Gensler asks whether the recommendation should also apply to swap execution facilities in the new swaps market structure.
Ketchum says the omission was not intentional; the same logic should apply to responsible entities under SEC/CFTC oversight.
