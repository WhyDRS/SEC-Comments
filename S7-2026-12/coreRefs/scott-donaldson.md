## Billimek / Williams front-running CAT example

https://www.sec.gov/enforcement-litigation/litigation-releases/lr-25595

SEC said staff used the Consolidated Audit Trail database to uncover Williams’s alleged trading and identify how he profited by front-running Billimek’s employer’s large trades

https://www.courtlistener.com/docket/66637027/united-states-securities-and-exchange-commission-v-billimek/

The front‑running case (Billimek/Williams) was one of the first examples of the SEC relying on the Consolidated Audit Trail. According to the complaint, the SEC reconstructed every order placed by Williams over a six‑year period using the CAT’s time‑stamped order and execution data. By matching Williams’s orders against the fund’s large trades, investigators discovered 1 697 “intraday round‑trip” trades in which Williams opened a position before the fund traded and closed the position after the fund’s trade had moved the price. The complaint notes that Williams’s opening trades were highly correlated with the direction of the fund’s trades and that the probability of this overlap occurring randomly was less than one in a trillion. CAT data also allowed the SEC to calculate that Williams’s overlapping trades yielded about $47.3 million in profits and had a 97 percent “win rate”—numbers that came from examining each time‑stamped order in the CAT. Contemporary reporting confirms that the SEC used the CAT to follow 1 697 intraday equity trades, compute the 97 percent win rate and show the improbability of the pattern, which provided decisive evidence of front‑running. In essence, the CAT’s unified, granular data let regulators link Williams’s trades to the fund’s orders across multiple venues and demonstrate that his profits were not a coincidence.


## Discord / Twitter influencer manipulation case

https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2022-221


, the social‑media stock‑manipulation case (Constantin et al.) does not appear to involve the CAT. The SEC’s press release and the 39‑page complaint focus on the defendants’ own social‑media posts and chat‑room conversations, alleging that they promoted stocks on Twitter and Discord while secretly selling into the buying pressure. The complaint details how the defendants coordinated their purchases, issued price‑targets and “alerts,” and then “dumped” their shares for profits. It does not mention the Consolidated Audit Trail, nor do subsequent summaries of the case. Instead, investigators relied on communications evidence and the defendants’ trading records to show that the promotions were deceptive. Thus, only the Billimek/Williams front‑running case is known to have used CAT data, while the social‑media influencer case was built on traditional evidence rather than the audit‑trail database.
