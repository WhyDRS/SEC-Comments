# 23-13396 High-Value Filing Analysis

Use this file as the filled extraction notes for the core CAT funding case. The entries below separate party positions from actual court holdings.

## `156 - opinion-issued/156-1 - opinion-issued.md`

- `claim`: ASA and Citadel challenged the SEC's 2023 CAT funding order. Their broader petition attacked CAT as unlawful and challenged the funding model as arbitrary, but the court resolved the case on the funding-order defects rather than on the legality of CAT itself.
- `who benefits`: ASA, Citadel, and broker-dealers benefited from vacatur of the 2023 funding model. SEC, CAT LLC, and SRO intervenors lost the approved funding order but received a short mandate stay to keep CAT funding from collapsing immediately.
- `money fact`: The 2023 executed-share model allocated historical and prospective CAT costs among three transaction-side payors: buy-side executing broker, sell-side executing broker, and the SRO involved in the transaction. The problem was that SROs could seek to pass their one-third share through to broker-dealers, so broker-dealers and their customers could effectively bear 100% of CAT costs.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The opinion describes CAT as a centralized audit trail collecting order and trade data that regulators use for surveillance, market reconstruction, enforcement, and oversight. The court did not hold that this collection is unlawful.
- `governance fact`: CAT is operated by SRO participants through CAT LLC. Broker-dealers pay or may pay a large portion of CAT costs but lack voting control over CAT LLC's budget and governance. The court treated that mismatch as important to the funding analysis because pass-through could leave broker-dealers paying the whole bill without budget control.
- `court holding`: The Eleventh Circuit held the 2023 Funding Order arbitrary and capricious because the SEC failed to explain or justify a major shift that allowed possible 100% pass-through of CAT costs to broker-dealers and failed to update its economic analysis around that possibility. The court vacated the order, remanded to the SEC, and stayed issuance of the mandate for 60 days. It did not decide whether CAT itself exceeds the SEC's statutory authority or violates the major questions doctrine.
- `comment use`: Use for Questions 4-13 on governance, advisory structure, budgeting discipline, and conflict management. Do not cite it as a holding that CAT is illegal; cite it as a holding that CAT funding governance must be reasoned, transparent, and tied to real cost incentives.

## `49 - appellant-s-brief-filed-by-ASA-and-citadel-23-1.md` and `102 - reply-brief-filed-by-petitioners-ASA-and-citadel-ll.md`

- `claim`: ASA and Citadel argued that CAT is an unprecedented surveillance system adopted without clear congressional authorization, that the SEC lacked authority to impose the CAT and its costs, and that the funding order unlawfully shifted costs onto broker-dealers through a structure that looked facially shared but functionally permitted full pass-through.
- `who benefits`: ASA, Citadel, and broker-dealers would benefit from vacatur, reduced CAT fees, refund or offset arguments, and a narrower regulator data regime. SEC, CAT LLC, SROs, and regulators would lose the stable funding mechanism and possibly the regulatory utility of a centralized audit trail if the broader statutory arguments were accepted.
- `money fact`: Petitioners emphasized that CAT development exceeded $1 billion and that annual operating costs were more than $200 million, framing CAT costs as close to a material fraction of the SEC's own budget. They also argued that SRO pass-through could convert a nominal two-thirds broker-dealer allocation into effective broker-dealer-only funding.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: Petitioners framed CAT as collecting granular trading histories and customer-identifying information at scale, with regulators able to query centralized order and account-linked information rather than making narrower case-by-case requests.
- `governance fact`: Petitioners stressed that SROs control CAT LLC and its budget while broker-dealers and investors lack voting control. They treated that structure as a conflict because SROs could approve or influence costs and then pass costs downstream.
- `court holding`: Party position only. The later opinion accepted the APA defect around pass-through and economic analysis but did not accept, reject, or resolve the broader CAT-validity and major-questions attacks.
- `comment use`: Use to describe the anti-CAT side's strongest framing: surveillance burden, industry tax, unchecked SRO budget incentives, and lack of direct broker-dealer governance. Pair with the opinion to avoid overstating what the court held.

## `96 - appellee-s-brief-filed-by-respondent-SEC-23-13396-ecf-d.md`

- `claim`: The SEC defended Rule 613, the CAT NMS Plan, and the 2023 funding amendment as lawful Exchange Act implementation. It argued that challenges to CAT itself were time-barred and that the funding model was reasonable, equitable, and supported by the record.
- `who benefits`: SEC, regulators, CAT LLC, and SROs benefit from the SEC's position because it preserves CAT as a regulator tool and preserves a route for broker-dealer contributions. Broker-dealers benefit only to the extent the SEC's review of individual fee filings remains a check against unreasonable fees.
- `money fact`: The SEC defended the executed-share allocation and argued that pass-through concerns were speculative or reviewable later through SRO fee filings. It also argued that overpayments could be accounted for through future fee adjustments.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The SEC emphasized CAT's market-surveillance value and pointed to privacy/security changes, including narrowed customer-identifying data and access controls. Its position was that regulator access to centralized data is a core oversight benefit, not a defect.
- `governance fact`: The SEC relied on Exchange Act and NMS Plan oversight: SRO plan amendments require SEC approval, and SRO fee filings remain subject to public comment, suspension, and Commission review.
- `court holding`: The court rejected the SEC's defense of the 2023 funding order on the pass-through explanation and economic-analysis issues. The court did not resolve the SEC's time-bar and CAT-authority defenses.
- `comment use`: Use to support a pro-CAT comment that preserves regulator access while accepting the need for clearer funding governance, fee transparency, and stronger cost-control explanations.

## `97 - intervenor-s-brief-filed-by-intervenor-CAT-23-13396-ecf-ian-gersh.md` and `98 - intervenor-s-brief-filed-by-intervenors-CBOE.md`

- `claim`: CAT LLC and SRO intervenors defended the executed-share model, argued that SRO fee pass-through was not automatically approved by the funding order, and stressed that any later pass-through would require separate fee filings subject to Exchange Act review.
- `who benefits`: CAT LLC and the SROs benefit from keeping the 2023 funding order in place. Regulators benefit from continuity of CAT operations. Broker-dealers benefit only if the fee-filing process actually constrains pass-through and cost growth.
- `money fact`: Intervenors emphasized that SROs had fronted hundreds of millions of dollars to build and operate CAT, and that the executed-share model placed one-third of costs on the transaction SRO plus one-third on each executing broker side. The SRO briefs also acknowledged FINRA as the key pass-through example.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: CAT LLC and SROs framed CAT as necessary infrastructure for SRO and SEC surveillance, market reconstruction, and oversight after legacy audit trails were retired.
- `governance fact`: The intervenor briefs highlight private SRO financial interests: exchanges and FINRA are CAT participants, operate through CAT LLC, have a direct interest in CAT cost recovery, and intervene to defend the funding order. That is the clearest source for showing that SROs are not neutral observers in CAT funding litigation.
- `court holding`: The court rejected the premise that later fee-filing review cured the SEC's failure to address 100% pass-through in the funding order itself. It also rejected remand without vacatur.
- `comment use`: Use for Questions 4-13 to show why governance must address SRO conflicts and financial incentives without weakening regulator access to CAT data.

## Stay Package: `112`, `116-2`, `119`, `120`, `121`, `124`, and `134`

- `claim`: Petitioners and supporting amici sought a stay or injunction to prevent CAT fee collection while the merits petition was pending, arguing that broker-dealers would suffer unrecoverable monetary harm. SEC, CAT LLC, and SROs opposed, arguing that future fees could be adjusted, the petitioners waited too long, and a stay would destabilize CAT funding.
- `who benefits`: A stay would benefit ASA, Citadel, Virtu, SIFMA members, and broker-dealers facing immediate CAT invoices. Denial benefited CAT LLC, SROs, SEC, and regulators relying on uninterrupted CAT operations.
- `money fact`: The stay briefing contains practical fee numbers: monthly collections, historical build costs, and arguments over whether broker-dealers would pay nonrefundable fees before review. The opposing side emphasized that SROs had already funded CAT for years and should not be forced to bear 100% of costs during litigation.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: Petitioners used the stay record to frame CAT as a broad database whose operation should not be funded through disputed fees. Opponents emphasized ongoing surveillance needs and the retirement of legacy audit trails.
- `governance fact`: The stay filings show the same incentive dispute in emergency-relief form: broker-dealers wanted protection against nonrefundable fee collection, while SRO/CAT interests wanted continuity and cost recovery.
- `court holding`: The Eleventh Circuit denied the stay/injunctive-relief motion in `134`. That denial did not decide the merits; the later opinion vacated the 2023 funding order.
- `comment use`: Use to show urgency and irreparable-harm framing, but treat the denial as procedural/interim rather than a merits endorsement of the funding model.

## Rehearing: `161` and `162 - rehearing-order-filed/162-2 - rehearing-order-filed.md`

- `claim`: CAT LLC sought panel rehearing after the opinion. Petitioners opposed rehearing.
- `who benefits`: Denial benefited ASA/Citadel and left the vacatur intact. CAT LLC and SROs lost their attempt to reopen the panel decision.
- `money fact`: The rehearing stage preserves the vacatur consequences for CAT funding and the need for a new or revised model.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: No new privacy holding.
- `governance fact`: The rehearing petition underscores CAT LLC's direct financial and operational stake in the funding order.
- `court holding`: The panel denied CAT LLC's petition for rehearing on September 22, 2025.
- `comment use`: Use to show finality of the 2025 panel result when explaining why later 2026 funding actions are responses to a live vacatur, not abstract policy reconsideration.
