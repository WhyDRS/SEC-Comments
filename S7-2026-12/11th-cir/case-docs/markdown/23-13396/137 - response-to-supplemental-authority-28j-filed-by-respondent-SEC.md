Am. Secs. Ass’n et al. v. SEC (No. 23-13396) 
 
Dear Mr. Smith: 
 
The en banc Fifth Circuit’s 9-8 decision in Alliance for Fair Board Recruitment v. 
SEC, No. 21-60626 (Dec. 11, 2024) (AFBR), does nothing to bolster petitioners’ 
untimely statutory authority challenge.     
First, AFBR does not support petitioners’ argument that the integration of 
regulatory data previously available from disparate sources into a single database 
implicates the major questions doctrine.  The majority invoked that doctrine only as 
“confirm[ation]” of its interpretation of the statute’s “ordinary meaning.”  Op. 31.  
Here, the ordinary meaning of the relevant Exchange Act provisions plainly 
encompasses the creation of a consolidated audit trail.  SEC Br. 44-47; SEC Stay 
Opp. 11-12.  Nor does AFBR diminish the significance of the lack of controversy 
surrounding CAT’s creation.  CAT did not emerge “through a process akin to adverse 
possession” (Op. 38), as petitioners imply.  SROs and broker-dealers participated 
extensively in its development, and the overwhelming consensus supporting its 
creation (and absence of any dispute over authority) undermines petitioners’ 
suggestion that CAT transforms the statute or the Commission’s role.   
Second, AFBR does not hold that the Commission’s authority under Section 
11A (15 U.S.C. 78k-1) is limited to “market-competition measures” that reduce 
“transaction costs.”  On the contrary, the majority recognized that “Congress gave 
SEC broad discretion to flesh out the contours of the [national market system].”  
Op. 27.  The text and history of the 1975 amendments confirm that Congress also 
intended the Commission to use its authority to ensure the adequate regulation of that 
system.  SEC Br. 44-47.

Finally, the Commission does not argue that its authority is limited only by 
“general references to the ‘public interest’ and ‘the protection of investors.’”  Section 
11A authorizes the Commission to “require [the SROs] to act jointly” only with 
respect to matters within the SROs’ “share[d] authority” under the Exchange Act, 
only in connection with the exercise of authority granted to the Commission 
elsewhere in the Act, and only in furtherance of specific findings and objectives.  
These are meaningful constraints, and the creation of CAT satisfies each of them.  
SEC Br. 44-47, 52-53; SEC Stay Opp. 11-12.
