## Interest In The Case

PTG says its members are directly affected because they trade their own capital and have no customer base to pass CAT costs through to. Its practical point is that principal trading firms absorb CAT fees directly, either as CAT charges or as higher fees imposed by SROs.

## Core Positions

1. The CAT and 2026 order exceed SEC statutory authority.

PTG argues Congress did not authorize the SEC to build and fund CAT through the national-market-system provisions, especially given CAT's scale and surveillance function.

Key references:

- Table of contents issue heading: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:81`
- Section 11A / `15 U.S.C. § 78k-1` discussion: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:335`
- Major questions framing: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:384`

2. The 2026 order has the same economic effect as the vacated 2023 order.

PTG argues the 2026 order still allows SROs to recover their share indirectly from broker-dealers by increasing existing fees. That makes the formal allocation hollow.

Key references:

- Indirect recovery by existing fees: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:301`
- Same economic effect as 2023 order: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:307`
- Broker-dealers, including PTG members, could fund the entire system: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:308`

3. The SEC failed to conduct required economic analysis.

PTG argues the SEC did not meaningfully analyze the consequences of its 2026 choice under the APA, Exchange Act, and Rule 613.

Key references:

- Economic-analysis issue heading: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:87`
- Failure to conduct required analysis: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:547`
- APA arbitrary-and-capricious framing: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:550`
- SEC did not do the required work: `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md:575`

4. CAT governance creates misaligned incentives.

PTG argues the CAT structure lets SROs set or drive the budget while broker-dealers pay, which PTG links to CAT cost overruns and weak cost discipline.

Key references:

## Comment Use

PTG is useful for showing that the anti-CAT funding position is not only Citadel's own litigation posture. It is also a principal-trading-firm position focused on cost absorption, budget discipline, and the inability of own-capital trading firms to pass CAT costs to customers.
