# 26-10936 High-Value Filing Analysis

Use this file for the current 2026 CAT funding-order challenge. This case connects the 2025 Eleventh Circuit vacatur to the live concept-release period.

## `20 - subitems/20-1 - motion-for-stay.md` and `48 - reply-to-response-to-motion-to-stay-pending-appeal-20-filed-by-petitioners-ASA.md`

- `claim`: ASA and Citadel argued that the 2026 funding order repeats the same core defect as the vacated 2023 order: it nominally allocates CAT costs across broker-dealers and SROs but leaves room for SROs to shift costs back to broker-dealers, especially through indirect pass-through mechanisms. They also argued CAT is unlawful under major-questions and statutory-authority theories and that a stay is needed to prevent unrecoverable broker-dealer payments.
- `who benefits`: ASA, Citadel, broker-dealers, and market makers benefit if the stay prevents immediate fee collection. SROs, CAT LLC, SEC, and regulators lose near-term funding certainty if the stay is granted.
- `money fact`: Petitioners claimed SROs had already collected more than $300 million from broker-dealers under the vacated 2023 order and warned that broker-dealers could lose hundreds of millions more before the 2026 order expires. They also challenged the use of broker-dealer-paid reserves as a buffer during litigation.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: Petitioners continued to frame CAT as a centralized surveillance database that gives regulators direct access to investor and trading information. In reply, they contrasted direct access to CAT with narrower targeted requests to SROs.
- `governance fact`: Petitioners argued that SRO claims of funding harm are a pressure tactic because SROs are still legally responsible for maintaining CAT. They also argued that SROs should not be allowed to threaten defunding while using CAT LLC and fee mechanisms to shift costs to broker-dealers.
- `court holding`: Stay-stage party position only. As of the expedited briefing order, the Eleventh Circuit had not resolved the merits of 26-10936.
- `comment use`: Use to show current industry arguments against the 2026 model, especially indirect pass-through, unrecoverable fees, reserve use, and claims that SRO funding threats distort SEC decisionmaking.

## `42 - response-to-motion-filed-by-petitioners-ASA-and-citadel.md`

- `claim`: The SEC opposed the stay, arguing the 2026 order reasonably responded to the 2025 opinion by prohibiting new direct pass-through fees, considering the possibility of broker-dealer-only funding, and establishing interim funding while the SEC conducts comprehensive CAT review.
- `who benefits`: SEC, CAT LLC, SROs, regulators, and market-surveillance users benefit from the SEC position because it preserves stable CAT operations through March 2028. Broker-dealers benefit only to the extent future fee adjustments or later review can correct overpayments.
- `money fact`: The SEC argued petitioners had never shown irreparable harm from the two-thirds share historically allocated to broker-dealers and that any overpayment could be handled through later fee adjustments. The SEC also emphasized CAT budget reductions and recent reforms.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The SEC pointed to recent approval of removing some personally identifiable information from CAT and to the ongoing concept-release/comprehensive-review process as reasons a stay was unnecessary.
- `governance fact`: The SEC argued that later indirect pass-through attempts would require separate review through the fee-filing process and that the 2026 order did not bless every possible indirect pass-through mechanism.
- `court holding`: Party position only. No merits ruling yet.
- `comment use`: Use to support a comment that separates two issues: preserve CAT access and oversight, while asking the SEC to make pass-through limits, budget review, and reserve treatment more concrete.

## `45 - response-to-motion-to-stay-pending-appeal-filed-by-petitioners-ASA.md`

- `claim`: The intervenor exchanges filed a short statement opposing the stay and expressly relied on SEC and CAT LLC opposition briefing rather than adding a separate substantive argument.
- `who benefits`: Intervenor exchanges and other SRO participants benefit from opposing a stay because CAT funding continuity protects them from bearing interim costs alone.
- `money fact`: No independent money analysis; the filing is important because it shows SRO/intervenor alignment against a stay.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: No independent privacy analysis.
- `governance fact`: The appearance lists Cboe, Nasdaq, NYSE, and related exchange entities as intervenor exchanges. This is useful for identifying which private SRO market operators are participating in defense of the CAT funding framework.
- `court holding`: Party/intervenor position only.
- `comment use`: Use as a clean citation for private exchange involvement and financial interest in the current funding challenge.

## `50 - appellant-s-brief-filed-by-ASA-and-citadel-26-1.md`

- `claim`: On the merits, ASA and Citadel argued that the 2026 order must be vacated because CAT itself lacks clear statutory authorization, the funding model unlawfully taxes broker-dealers, the SEC failed to cure the 2023 pass-through defect, and the SEC failed to address the hundreds of millions allegedly collected under the vacated order.
- `who benefits`: Petitioners and broker-dealers benefit if the order is vacated or narrowed. SEC, CAT LLC, SROs, and regulators benefit if the order is upheld.
- `money fact`: The brief attacks the continued use of the executed-share approach and the treatment of prior collections/reserves. It argues the SEC is allowing CAT to operate on broker-dealer money during the comprehensive review rather than forcing SROs to carry the interim cost.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The brief renews the argument that CAT's data collection and direct regulator access raise major privacy and surveillance concerns that Congress did not clearly authorize.
- `governance fact`: Petitioners treat the SRO/CAT LLC structure as a private-cost-shifting mechanism: SROs operate and govern CAT, SEC approves the model, and broker-dealers are asked to fund most or all of it without equivalent control.
- `court holding`: Merits briefing position only. The case is still on an expedited schedule.
- `comment use`: Use as the current anti-CAT merits roadmap. Pair with 23-13396 to distinguish what has already been held from what petitioners are still trying to win.

## `47 - court-order-filed/47-2 - court-order-filed.md`

- `claim`: The parties jointly sought and received an expedited merits schedule.
- `who benefits`: All parties benefit from a faster merits path; petitioners benefit because fee collection is ongoing, and SEC/CAT/SROs benefit because expedited review can reduce uncertainty around interim funding.
- `money fact`: The expedited schedule matters because the disputed 2026 funding order covers an interim period while fees and reserves are being used.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: No privacy holding.
- `governance fact`: The order places merits briefing on a tight track: petitioners' initial brief due May 21, 2026; petitioner-side amici due May 28; SEC/intervenor responses due July 2; respondent-side amici due July 9; reply due July 23; oral argument after August 2026.
- `court holding`: The court granted the joint motion to set an expedited briefing schedule. It did not decide the stay or merits in this order.
- `comment use`: Use to show the current case is recent and not far along on the merits; any concept-release comment should treat 26-10936 as active litigation rather than settled law.

## `amicus-briefs/54 - brief/54-2 - brief.md` and `ptg-markets-amicus-dossier.md`

- `claim`: PTG Markets appeared as an amicus supporting petitioners and framed the 2026 order as a live burden on proprietary trading firms and market makers.
- `who benefits`: PTG and similar proprietary trading firms benefit from stay/vacatur arguments that delay or reduce CAT fees. SEC, CAT LLC, and SROs benefit from defending the interim funding model.
- `money fact`: The amicus position is useful for showing that the broker-dealer/market-maker objection is not limited to Citadel, although Citadel remains the named petitioner.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: Treat any privacy claims in the amicus brief as industry position, not court finding.
- `governance fact`: The amicus appearance broadens the record of industry participants challenging CAT funding and reinforces that CAT fee disputes affect market makers beyond Citadel.
- `court holding`: Amicus position only.
- `comment use`: Use to answer whether other market makers or brokers are challenging the CAT funding model and to map aligned industry interests.
