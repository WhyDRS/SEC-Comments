# 1-26-cv-00134 High-Value Filing Analysis

Use this file for the D.D.C. parallel challenge over CAT LLC's use of reserve funds after the 2023 funding order was vacated. This is not an Eleventh Circuit merits appeal, but it is tightly related to the 2026 CAT funding fight.

## `5 - preliminary-injunction/5-1 - memorandum-in-support.md`

- `claim`: Citadel sought a preliminary injunction barring CAT LLC from spending or depleting its reserve until the SEC reviewed the reserve issue. Citadel argued that CAT LLC's spend-down of funds collected under the vacated 2023 order is an unsupervised private exercise of delegated regulatory power under `Alpine`.
- `who benefits`: Citadel and other broker-dealers benefit if the reserve is preserved for refunds, offsets, or SEC-directed treatment. CAT LLC, SROs, SEC, and regulators lose near-term operating flexibility if the reserve is frozen.
- `money fact`: Citadel alleged CAT LLC held about $119 million in reserve, planned to deplete it by around August 2026, and had collected hundreds of millions under the vacated 2023 order, including more than $56 million from Citadel. Citadel also claimed roughly $16 million of the reserve was attributable to Citadel and roughly $9.7 million represented inflated reserve overage.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The motion frames CAT as a surveillance system but the immediate requested relief targets reserve spending, not data access. Citadel argues CAT can keep operating without using broker-dealer-paid reserves because SROs historically funded CAT.
- `governance fact`: Citadel's governance theory is that CAT LLC and SRO operators are spending regulatory fees before SEC review can occur, making later oversight meaningless. Citadel uses `Alpine` to argue that delayed SEC review is too little when the private SRO-affiliated entity's action has irreversible consequences.
- `court holding`: Party position only. The memorandum requests preliminary relief; it is not a ruling.
- `comment use`: Use for reserve-governance questions: who controls reserve funds, whether reserve drawdowns need prior SEC approval, and how to prevent spend-down from mooting later review.

## `20 - memorandum-in-opposition-to-motion/20 - memorandum-in-opposition-to-motion.md` and `21 - memorandum-in-opposition-to-motion/21 - memorandum-in-opposition-to-motion.md`

- `claim`: CAT LLC opposed the preliminary injunction, arguing that the SEC had already approved reserve collection and use through the 2016 CAT NMS Plan, 2023 funding order, and subsequent fee filings, and that Citadel was trying to bypass SEC review and court-of-appeals review channels.
- `who benefits`: CAT LLC, SROs, SEC, regulators, and public market-surveillance users benefit if the injunction is denied. Citadel and broker-dealers benefit if the court rejects CAT LLC's view and preserves the reserve.
- `money fact`: CAT LLC stated that the 2023 funding order authorized a reserve of up to 25% of the annual budget and that unexpected surplus above that level should offset future fees. It cited a 2025 operating budget around $249 million, later cost reductions, an estimated 2026 operating budget around $156 million, a projected reserve starting balance around $119 million, and SRO loans approaching $1 billion over CAT's buildout.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: CAT LLC emphasized that the CAT is the active market-oversight tool on which the SEC and SROs rely after legacy audit trails were retired. It argued that freezing reserve use could create uncertainty over CAT operations and market surveillance.
- `governance fact`: CAT LLC's central governance defense is that there is no SEC oversight gap: CAT NMS Plan amendments, fee filings, budget materials, reserve treatment, and rulemaking petitions are all subject to SEC control or review. It argued the SEC could act through Rule 608(d), the pending funding proposal, an interim model, exemptive authority, Citadel's petition for rulemaking, or its own CAT NMS Plan amendments.
- `court holding`: Party position only. These opposition briefs ask the court to deny the preliminary injunction.
- `comment use`: Use as the strongest defense of current CAT reserve governance. It supports a comment that the SEC has tools, but also identifies the exact tools that can be made more explicit or mandatory in the concept-release response.

## `22 - reply-to-opposition-to-motion/22 - reply-to-opposition-to-motion.md`

- `claim`: Citadel replied that CAT LLC was spending unlawfully collected funds before the SEC could act, that general SEC oversight is insufficient under `Alpine`, and that the 2016 CAT NMS Plan did not authorize spending reserves generated under a vacated funding order.
- `who benefits`: Citadel, broker-dealers, and fee challengers benefit from this framing because it treats reserve preservation as necessary to meaningful SEC review. CAT LLC and SROs lose if reserve use is frozen pending SEC action.
- `money fact`: Citadel reiterated that CAT LLC collected hundreds of millions under the vacated 2023 order, including over $56 million from Citadel, and was spending nearly $15 million per month from a reserve of nearly $120 million.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The reply keeps the focus on funding and reserve spend-down, not data removal or access. It argues that the CAT can continue because SROs are legally required to run it and have historically done so without broker-dealer fee collections.
- `governance fact`: Citadel argued the key problem is timing: if CAT LLC can drain the reserve before the SEC rules, SEC review becomes largely academic. It rejected CAT LLC's view that available SEC tools are enough without an automatic pause.
- `court holding`: Party position only. The reply asks for the preliminary injunction.
- `comment use`: Use to frame concrete reform proposals: reserve drawdowns should be transparent, bounded, and subject to prior SEC approval or automatic stay when challenged.
