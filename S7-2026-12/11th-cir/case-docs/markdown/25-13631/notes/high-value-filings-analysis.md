# 25-13631 High-Value Filing Analysis

Use this file for the IEX Options/order-protection case. It is not a CAT funding challenge, but it is useful context for Citadel's broader market-structure litigation posture.

## `71 - opinion-issued/71-1 - opinion-issued.md`

- `claim`: Citadel challenged the SEC's approval of IEX Options, arguing that IEX's 350-microsecond speed bump and order-repricing protection were unsupported, unfairly discriminatory, inconsistent with protected-quotation rules, and harmful to competition.
- `who benefits`: IEX, SEC, investors harmed by latency arbitrage, and market makers seeking protection from stale-quote pickoffs benefited from the denial of Citadel's petition. Citadel and similarly positioned high-speed trading firms lost their challenge.
- `money fact`: The opinion treats latency arbitrage as economically meaningful and cites estimates that investors lose more than $5 billion per year to latency arbitrage. This is not a CAT fee case.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The opinion does not turn on privacy. Its CAT relevance is indirect: Citadel argued in briefing that the SEC should have used CAT options data to evaluate latency arbitrage and IEX's proposal, while the SEC and court accepted other record evidence.
- `governance fact`: The case concerns SEC approval of an exchange rule filing by IEX. The governance issue is the SEC's authority to approve market-structure reforms over objections by a large market maker.
- `court holding`: The Eleventh Circuit denied Citadel's petition. It held that substantial evidence supported the SEC's findings that latency arbitrage exists in options markets and that IEX's mechanism targets it; that IEX Options quotes can be protected; that the rule is not unfairly discriminatory; and that the SEC reasonably found no undue burden on competition.
- `comment use`: Use to show that Citadel's litigation is not confined to CAT funding or privacy. The same firm is challenging market-structure reforms that reduce speed advantages or alter market-maker economics.

## `26 - opening-brief-for-petitioner.md` and `59 - reply-brief-for-petitioner.md`

- `claim`: Citadel argued that IEX Options gives IEX market makers an asymmetric advantage by allowing quotes to be repriced or cancelled while incoming orders are delayed, yet still treats those quotes as protected and routes customer orders toward them. Citadel claimed the SEC relied on incomplete analogies to equities and ignored available CAT data.
- `who benefits`: Citadel and other latency-sensitive trading firms benefit if the IEX approval is vacated. Broker routers and competing exchanges may benefit from avoiding compulsory routing to IEX quotes. IEX and market makers seeking anti-latency-arbitrage protection would lose.
- `money fact`: Citadel framed the proposal as shifting trading economics by attracting order flow and transaction/data-fee revenue to IEX, not as a direct fee assessment. Its brief also cited market-maker costs of maintaining latency-competitive infrastructure.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: Citadel invoked CAT as a data source, arguing that CAT records every options order and could identify retail activity. That is important for the concept release because Citadel elsewhere attacks CAT as surveillance, while here it argues the SEC should have used CAT data for market-structure review.
- `governance fact`: Citadel challenged how the SEC handled the rule-approval record, especially whether the SEC could approve a market reform without obtaining additional data that Citadel believed necessary.
- `court holding`: Party position only. The court rejected Citadel's arguments in the opinion.
- `comment use`: Use to show strategic inconsistency: CAT data is criticized as overbroad surveillance in CAT litigation but invoked as the data source the SEC should use when Citadel attacks a reform it dislikes.

## `39 - appellee-brief.md`

- `claim`: The SEC defended its IEX approval as supported by record evidence, comments from market participants, prior experience with IEX's equities speed bump, and the Exchange Act's competition and investor-protection standards.
- `who benefits`: SEC, IEX, investors, and market makers harmed by latency arbitrage benefit from the SEC's defense. Citadel and speed-advantaged liquidity takers benefit if the defense fails.
- `money fact`: The SEC relied on evidence that latency arbitrage creates costly technological arms races and harms liquidity provision. It treated the reform as likely to improve competition and investor outcomes rather than impose unjustified costs.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: The SEC responded that CAT data's existence did not require the agency to build a new empirical model from raw CAT data before approving IEX's proposal. That position limits how far CAT must be used in every market-structure order.
- `governance fact`: The SEC argued that exchange rule approval can rely on substantial evidence, expert judgment, comments, and market experience; it need not satisfy Citadel's preferred data protocol.
- `court holding`: The court largely accepted the SEC's position and denied the petition.
- `comment use`: Use to argue for regulator access to CAT data without turning CAT into a litigation trap where agencies must run every conceivable CAT analysis before approving reforms.

## `50 - intervenor-brief-filed.md`

- `claim`: IEX defended its model as a targeted response to latency arbitrage, not a market-maker giveaway. It argued the speed bump and quote-repricing logic protect liquidity providers from stale-quote pickoffs and can improve displayed liquidity and competition.
- `who benefits`: IEX and market participants who value anti-latency-arbitrage design benefit. Investors benefit if the mechanism improves liquidity and reduces predatory speed-based trading. Citadel loses if its speed advantage is constrained.
- `money fact`: IEX framed latency arbitrage as a source of market-wide cost and a driver of expensive speed investments, rather than as productive competition.
- `surveillance/privacy fact`: No direct privacy issue. CAT relevance is contextual only.
- `governance fact`: IEX's appearance as intervenor shows that exchange rule litigation can include direct participation by private market-structure actors whose business models are affected by SEC approval decisions.
- `court holding`: Party position only. The court's opinion accepted the core SEC/IEX defense.
- `comment use`: Use for the broader narrative that resistance to CAT should be evaluated alongside resistance to reforms aimed at speed-based trading advantages.
