Yeah. I’d avoid saying **“the stock market is a public place”** because that sounds like a privacy-tort/photo case analogy. Stronger phrasing is:

> The public securities markets are **impersonal, federally regulated public markets** where prices are formed through publicly disseminated information.

Best authorities:

**Core Supreme Court cite:**

> **Basic Inc. v. Levinson, 485 U.S. 224, 246–47 (1988).**

Use this for the idea that public stock trading occurs in an **“impersonal” market** and that most publicly available information is reflected in market price. The Court ties the Exchange Act to the idea of a “free and open public market” where competing judgments set a fair price. ([Justia Law][1])






**Circuit cites for the same market-as-public-information system:**


> **In re Xcelera.com Sec. Litig., 430 F.3d 503, 511–12 (1st Cir. 2005)** / **In re PolyMedica Corp. Sec. Litig., 432 F.3d 1 (1st Cir. 2005).**

Good for: efficient market = stock price reflects all publicly available information, rapidly. 




> **Teamsters Local 445 Freight Div. Pension Fund v. Bombardier Inc., 546 F.3d 196, 204–05 (2d Cir. 2008).**

Good for: efficient market means prices incorporate most public information rapidly. ([Jenner & Block LLP | Law Firm - Homepage][2])






> **In re BofI Holding, Inc. Sec. Litig., 977 F.3d 781, 794–97 (9th Cir. 2020).**

Good for: public reports, blog posts, lawsuits, and market reaction in publicly traded stock. It treats the market as the audience, but adds that not every public accusation credibly reveals truth to the market. 




> **Carpenters Pension Fund of Ill. v. MiMedx Grp., Inc., 73 F.4th 1220, 1246–47 (11th Cir. 2023).**

Good for: public information already known to the market generally cannot be repackaged as a new corrective disclosure. 




> **Defeo v. IonQ, Inc., No. 24-1709 (4th Cir. Apr. 8, 2025).**

Good recent Fourth Circuit support for the same “revealed to the market” framing in a public-company/short-seller context, especially if you are discussing public-market commentary and price reaction. ([Paul, Weiss][3])




For ownership/privacy specifically, also useful:

> **Exchange Act §§ 13(d), 13(g) / Schedules 13D and 13G.**

SEC says investors owning more than 5% of a covered class of public equity securities must publicly file Schedule 13D or 13G, which helps show that public-company ownership positions can carry mandatory public-disclosure consequences. ([SEC][4])




Draft sentence:

> Public securities markets are not private enclaves; they are impersonal, regulated public markets in which participants trade at prices formed through public information. See **Basic Inc. v. Levinson**, 485 U.S. 224, 246–47 (1988); **Teamsters Local 445 v. Bombardier Inc.**, 546 F.3d 196, 204–05 (2d Cir. 2008); **In re BofI Holding, Inc. Sec. Litig.**, 977 F.3d 781, 794–97 (9th Cir. 2020).

Caveat: that supports **public-market information / commentary / price-impact** arguments. It does **not** automatically mean there is no privacy interest in all customer-identifying brokerage records, CAT data, or nonpublic order information.
